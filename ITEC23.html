Introduction

My journey through ITEC 23 has fundamentally transformed my understanding of technology's societal impact and professional responsibilities

In our increasingly digital world, information technology has become the backbone of modern society. My exploration in ITEC 23 - Social and Professional Issues in Information Technology has revealed the profound ethical dimensions that underpin every technological decision we make as IT professionals. This portfolio represents my intellectual and ethical journey through the complex landscape where technology meets human values.

What began as academic requirement has evolved into a personal mission. The course exposed me to critical issues like algorithmic bias, surveillance capitalism, and the ethical implications of artificial intelligence. These aren't abstract concepts—they're real challenges affecting real people, from the individual whose data is harvested without consent to communities marginalized by biased algorithms.

The real problem of humanity is that we have Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, and god-like technology.

— E.O. Wilson

Through case studies like Cambridge Analytica's data exploitation and the ethical dilemmas of self-driving cars, I've come to understand that technical proficiency alone is insufficient. True professionalism in IT requires what philosopher Luciano Floridi calls "information ethics"—a framework that considers the moral implications of how we create, store, distribute, and access information.

Case Study Analysis

A deep examination of the 2023 Medusa Ransomware Attacks through multiple ethical lenses

Medusa Ransomware Attacks on Educational Institutions

2023 Cybersecurity Crisis - Ethical Analysis

4.1 Incident Summary

Between March and June 2023, the Medusa ransomware group launched targeted attacks against over 50 educational institutions across North America and Europe. The attacks followed a consistent pattern: exploitation of unpatched vulnerabilities in school management systems, encryption of critical data (including student records, financial documents, and research databases), and demands ranging from $50,000 to $1 million in cryptocurrency for decryption keys.

The impact was devastating. Several universities had to postpone exams when they lost access to digital testing systems. A community college in Ohio reported losing 15 years of archival research. Most tragically, special education schools faced disruptions to individualized education programs (IEPs), directly impacting vulnerable students.

4.2 Ethical Issues Involved

The Medusa attacks presented multiple ethical dimensions that warrant careful analysis:

  • Exploitation of vulnerable institutions: Schools were targeted precisely because they're often under-resourced yet data-rich, raising questions about distributive justice
  • Student privacy violations: Encryption held hostage sensitive data including mental health records, disability accommodations, and financial aid information
  • Educational equity impacts: Attacks disproportionately affected public institutions and community colleges over wealthy private universities
  • Ethical dilemma of ransom payments: While paying ransoms funds criminal enterprises, refusal could harm students through lost records
  • Professional responsibility: Questions about whether IT staff adequately prioritized security given known vulnerabilities

Cybersecurity is much more than a matter of IT. It's about protecting our way of life.

— Stephane Nappo

4.3 Application of Ethical Theories

Analyzing the Medusa attacks through different ethical frameworks reveals distinct perspectives on responsibility and appropriate responses:

Deontological Analysis

From a Kantian perspective, the attacks were categorically wrong regardless of consequences, violating universal moral laws against theft and harm. The attackers treated educational institutions merely as means to financial ends, not as ends in themselves. This framework would argue against paying ransoms, as it would encourage future attacks and violate the categorical imperative.

Utilitarian Perspective

A consequentialist analysis would weigh the net harm caused—not just financial losses but educational disruptions, mental health impacts on students, and research setbacks. Some utilitarians might reluctantly support paying ransoms if it minimized overall harm, though this risks creating perverse incentives. The attacks revealed systemic vulnerabilities that utilitarians would prioritize fixing.

Virtue Ethics Examination

This approach would focus on the character and intentions of all parties. The attackers demonstrated vices like greed and callousness toward education's social value. IT professionals might reflect on whether they exhibited sufficient prudence and diligence in system maintenance. School administrators would need courage to make difficult decisions about ransom payments.

Social Contract Theory

From this perspective, the attacks violated the implicit social contract that enables educational institutions to operate. There's an argument that society has failed to adequately protect these vital institutions. The incidents reveal gaps in our collective responsibility to safeguard education from digital threats.

4.4 Proposed Ethical Solutions

Addressing the systemic issues revealed by the Medusa attacks requires multi-layered solutions that address technical, organizational, and policy dimensions:

Technical Safeguards
  • Implementation of immutable backups stored air-gapped from primary systems
  • Adoption of zero-trust architectures to limit lateral movement during breaches
  • Regular penetration testing with ethical hackers to identify vulnerabilities
Policy Interventions
  • Creation of a federal cybersecurity grant program for educational institutions
  • Development of sector-specific incident response protocols
  • International cooperation to track and disrupt ransomware payment channels
Educational Initiatives
  • Cybersecurity training programs for non-technical school administrators
  • Integration of ethical hacking concepts into computer science curricula
  • Public awareness campaigns about ransomware risks and prevention
Ethical Considerations
  • Development of ethical guidelines for ransomware response scenarios
  • Creation of alternative dispute resolution mechanisms for cyberattacks
  • Establishment of cyber insurance pools to reduce pressure to pay ransoms

The only truly secure system is one that is powered off, cast in a block of concrete and sealed in a lead-lined room with armed guards.

— Gene Spafford

This case study has profoundly influenced my career aspirations. I now plan to specialize in educational technology security, helping institutions protect their systems without compromising accessibility or pedagogical mission. The Medusa attacks demonstrate that cybersecurity isn't just about technology—it's about safeguarding society's foundational institutions.

Professional Ethics Pledge

My personal commitment to ethical practice in information technology

Ethics is knowing the difference between what you have a right to do and what is right to do.

— Potter Stewart

As I prepare to enter the information technology profession, I recognize that technical competence alone is insufficient. The power we wield as IT professionals comes with profound responsibilities—to individuals, organizations, and society at large. This pledge represents my personal code of conduct, synthesizing the lessons from this course into actionable commitments for my career.

Guardian of Privacy
I will treat all personal data as a sacred trust, implementing security measures that exceed legal minimums and advocating for privacy-by-design in all systems I help create.
Inspired by the EU's GDPR and the principles of data minimization, this commitment reflects my belief that privacy is foundational to human dignity in the digital age.
Champion of Equity
I will actively work to identify and mitigate biases in algorithms and systems, ensuring technology serves all communities fairly regardless of race, gender, or socioeconomic status.
The work of Joy Buolamwini on algorithmic bias demonstrated how technology can perpetuate inequality when not consciously designed for inclusion.
Advocate for Transparency
I will resist pressures to obscure system limitations or risks, providing clear explanations of technology's capabilities and potential impacts to all stakeholders.
The Theranos scandal revealed the dangers of "fake it till you make it" culture in tech—transparency builds trust and prevents harm.
Lifelong Learner
I will dedicate at least 100 hours annually to studying emerging ethical challenges in IT, recognizing that moral understanding must evolve alongside technology.
As expressed by philosopher Luciano Floridi, information ethics requires continuous adaptation to new technological contexts.
Steward of Society
I will consider the broader societal impacts of my work, advocating for projects that benefit the public good and resisting those that may cause harm.
The IEEE Code of Ethics reminds us that technology professionals have obligations to society that transcend employer interests.
Defender of Security
I will prioritize system security not as an afterthought but as a fundamental requirement, protecting users from harm through diligent practice.
The Medusa attacks demonstrated how security failures can disproportionately impact society's most vulnerable institutions.

By upholding these principles, I commit to being not just a skilled technologist, but an ethical professional who contributes to human flourishing through responsible innovation.
